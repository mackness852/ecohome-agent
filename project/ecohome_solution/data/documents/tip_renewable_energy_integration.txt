Right-size your solar array to your actual load profile, not maximum possible output. Excess generation you can't use or store is wasted.

Install an energy monitor before you install panels. Know your consumption curve first — most household load falls in the evening, outside solar hours.

Use a diverter controller to redirect surplus solar to immersion heaters or EV chargers instead of exporting at low feed-in rates. Payback is often under a year.

Pair panels with a heat pump rather than resistance heating. Heat pumps deliver 3–4 kWh of heat per 1 kWh consumed; resistance heaters deliver 1:1.

Consider an east-west panel split rather than pure south-facing. Lower peak output but generation spreads across more hours, improving self-consumption without storage.

Monitor inverter performance weekly. A single failed panel can silently drag down an entire string without triggering any alarm.

Register for the Smart Export Guarantee (UK). Rates vary by supplier — review annually, they've shifted significantly.

Add small-scale wind generation in locations with genuine wind resource. Wind complements solar seasonally, particularly in Scottish winters.

Explore virtual power plant or aggregator schemes. Some suppliers pay you to discharge or charge your battery at grid-optimal moments.

Plan for panel degradation of 0.5–0.7% per year. Output will be roughly 15% lower by year 25 — size your array accordingly, not just for year one.
